# Calibration Rules

These rules guide how candidate signals are interpreted across roles at Tillo.

They support consistent progression decisions across teams and reduce reliance on checklist matching.


---

# Progression Philosophy

Recruiting at Tillo is alignment work between:

- candidate capability
- team context
- business direction

Progression decisions should reflect signal strength rather than completeness of match.


---

# Candidate Classification Model

Candidates typically fall into one of three groups.


## Strong alignment candidates

Evidence includes:

- ownership of outcomes
- appropriate delivery scope
- relevant stakeholder exposure
- execution consistency

These candidates normally progress.


## Comparison profiles

Evidence includes:

- partial alignment with role expectations
- narrower scope than target level
- environment differences
- unclear signals requiring validation

These candidates help calibrate decisions but do not automatically progress.


## Weak alignment candidates

Evidence includes:

- limited ownership signals
- support-only delivery history
- unclear contribution to outcomes
- mismatch with role scope expectations

These candidates do not progress.


---

# Acceptable Gaps

Missing experience should be interpreted relative to context rather than treated as automatic rejection signals.

Examples of commonly acceptable gaps include:

- unfamiliar tools or frameworks
- adjacent platform environments
- different domain exposure where domain expertise is not critical

Role playbooks define where gaps are acceptable or critical.


---

# Domain Experience Interpretation

Domain familiarity expectations differ by role family.

Often important in:

- product roles
- commercial roles
- operations roles
- partnerships roles
- marketing roles

Often less critical in:

- engineering roles
- people roles

Role playbooks define where domain exposure is required.


---

# Ownership Signals Override Tool Matching

Evidence of delivery responsibility carries more weight than familiarity with specific technologies or platforms.

Prioritise signals such as:

- initiative ownership
- system responsibility
- release ownership
- delivery improvement contributions


---

# Environment Maturity Interpretation

Candidates from smaller or earlier-stage environments may still align strongly if they demonstrate:

- ownership of outcomes
- system improvement contributions
- delivery acceleration impact

Environment differences should guide validation questions rather than immediate rejection.


---

# Scale-up Signal Strength Indicators

Increase signal strength when candidates demonstrate:

- introducing structure into delivery environments
- improving systems rather than maintaining them
- owning releases or initiatives
- mentoring others
- introducing testing frameworks
- introducing CI/CD improvements
- modernising legacy systems
- improving delivery velocity
- working in ownership-heavy environments


Reduce signal strength when candidates demonstrate:

- primarily ticket-based implementation work
- agency-only delivery without ownership
- limited ability to explain system decisions
- limited ability to explain scaling decisions
- limited ability to explain trade-offs


---

# Ambiguity Handling Expectations

Successful candidates typically demonstrate comfort operating in:

- partially defined delivery environments
- evolving problem spaces
- cross-functional collaboration contexts

Lower ambiguity tolerance should be explored before progression decisions.


---

# Decision Authority Calibration

Level alignment should prioritise:

- initiative ownership
- system responsibility
- stakeholder alignment surface area
- decision-making authority

rather than job titles or tenure.


---

# When to Progress With Validation

Candidates should progress with targeted validation when:

- ownership signals are strong but environment differs
- scope signals are strong but stakeholder exposure differs
- domain exposure differs but role playbook allows flexibility
- execution pace signals are still forming


---

# When Not to Progress

Candidates typically do not progress when:

- ownership signals are absent
- delivery scope is consistently below role expectations
- contributions cannot be clearly explained
- decision authority history is unclear
